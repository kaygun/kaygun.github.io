The Kitchen Sink and Other Oddities

Atabey Kaygun

A topology problem

Description of the problem

Today, I am going to consider a topology problem: If X is a compact normal topological space, and if ∼ is an equivalence relation on X, under what conditions is the quotient space X/∼ of equivalence classes also compact normal? Here is one possible solution.

Assume (X,τ) is a compact normal (T4+T1) topological space, (Y,κ) be a T1 topological space, and let π: X → Y be a surjective continuous map. Then Y is also compact normal.

For example this works when each equivalence class is closed.

Proof

Image of a compact subset K of X is compact.

Assume K ⊆ X is compact, and consider π(K) ⊆ Y. Take an open cover 𝒰 of π(K). Then π−1𝒰 = {π−1(U)∣U∈𝒰} is an open cover of π−1π(K). However, since K ⊆ π−1π(K), we also have that π−1𝒰 is an open cover of K. But K is compact. So, there is a finite subcover π−1𝒰′. Since ππ−1(U) ⊆ U for every U, we get that 𝒰′ is also a finite subcover of π(K).

Y is compact

Easily follows from the fact that X is compact and π(X) = Y.

Every closed subset K of X is compact.

Assume 𝒰 is an open cover of K. Then 𝒰′ = 𝒰 ∪ {Kc} is an open cover of X, but X is compact. So, 𝒰′ has a finite subcover $\mathcal{U}"$. Then $\mathcal{U}"\setminus\left\{K^c\right\}$ is a finite subcover of K.

Every compact subset K of X is closed.

Fix y ∈ Kc. Since X is T2 any point a ∈ K and y ∈ Kc can be separated by open neighborhoods which means for all a ∈ K, there are open subset Ua, Va ∈ τ where a ∈ Ua and y ∈ Va and Ua ∩ Va is empty. Then 𝒰 = {Ua}a ∈ K is an open cover of K. Since K is compact, 𝒰 has a finite subcover. Then there are finitely many points a1, …, an ∈ K such that $$ K\subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^n U_{a_i}\quad \text{ and } \quad y\in V = \bigcap_{i=1}^n V_{a_i} $$ and V ∩ K is empty. Thus Kc is open, or equivalently, K is closed.

π is a closed map.

In other words, for every closed subset K ⊆ X, the image π(K) ⊆ Y is closed. This easily follows from the fact that K ⊆ X is closed if and only if K is compact, and π sends compact sets to compact sets.

Y is normal

Assume A1 and A2 are two disjoint closed subsets of Y. Then π−1(Ai) for i = 1, 2 are two disjoint closed subsets of X. Since X is normal, there are two disjoint open subsets Ui for i = 1, 2 such that π−1(Ai) ⊆ Ui. Then since π is surjective we get Aic = ππ−1(Aic) ⊇ π(Uic). Since π is a closed map, π(Uic) is closed, and therefore, π(Uic)c is open with the property that Ai ⊆ π(Uic)c. All that remains is to check the disjointness of π(U1c)c and π(U2c)c. We get π(U1c)c ∩ π(U2c)c = (π(U1c)∪π(U2c))c = (π(U1c∪U2c))c = π(X)c = ∅ since U1 ∩ U2 is the empty set.

Extension

We can do with less. In fact, the whole proof relies on the fact that π is a closed surjection. So, here is one possible extension: Assume (X,τ) is T4 + T1 and (Y,κ) is T1. Assume also that π: X → Y is a closed continuous surjection. Then Y is also a T4 + T1 space.
